Aerodynamic Design

Duration - January 9, 2020 - January 28, 2020
Course - Principles of Engineering
Objective - To design and construct a PVC launcher compatible projectile that flies the furthest
Type of Project - 4 Person Group

For this project, our group was tasked with designing and building a projectile made entirely out of paper. After the projectile was made, it would then be attached to a pressurized PVC pipe which would then launch it through the air. To create the body of the plane, I used rolled up printer paper reinforced with 3 layers of tape for a light, yet rigid body. My initial design was a glider that had paper wings with manila supports and a pressure cap made out of manila as well. However, I soon realized that a glider was not the optimal design for a pressurized air launcher. This was because the addition of large wings introduces a large margin of error that can stem from the turbulence during the launch, which will almost always make the glider spin in a spiral. As a result, I decided to go with a standard rocket with a long body and small trapezoidal fins. My new projectile had a 14.8-inch body (15-inch limit) to maximize its initial speed on launch. The choice of trapezoidal fins meant that there was not a significant amount of drag, but it still provided both high stability and rigidity — something essential to maintaining the ideal arc. I chose to go with relatively small fins as I knew that the projectile would be in a spiral, so thus wanted to limit the amount of momentum loss by the fins, but at the same time I still wanted enough resistance to maintain stability and reduce yaw movement. My second rocket was pretty close to the theoretical horizontal displacement (354 ft vs 353.7 ft) due to its sleek design which limited the effect air resistance had on it. There was still some deviation which could be attributed to slight yaw movement from the small fins. Although the fins were small and didn’t have that much drag, it was a tradeoff between stability and drag. This is because there isn’t a lot of problems it faces when it comes into contact with external factors, such as wind. The projectile is designed for distance and maintaining the ideal path — something that it does well. Some modifications for future flights could be the addition of a weighted cone to further shift the center of mass forward, reduce drag on the body through better taping (no air bubbles), and fine-tuning fins to reduce drag but also to maximize stability. Solar Hydrogen Vehicle

Duration - October 31, 2019 - November 18, 2019
Course - Principles of Engineering
Objective - To design and construct a vehicle that can travel 1 meter using hydrogen or solar energy
Type of Project - 4 Person Group

For this project, our group was tasked with creating a vehicle using either VEX or Fisher Tech parts that would run on either hydrogen fuel cells or solar fuel cells. The vehicle was required to fit within a certain parameter (5 x 12 inch), and be able to securely hold both the fuel cells and the breadboard when moving. For the best configuration, I decided to use a parallel circuit with 2 solar cells that would supply power to our VEX 393 motor, which would then turn a 1 : 6 sprocket system that provides the vehicle with more speed at the expense of torque. The goal of this project was to provide insight to a fictitious company in regard to the capabilities of the two energy sources, which supported through a series of tests involving a force measurer, timer, multimeter, and a meter stick. After the vehicle was fully built, a series of tests were run to determine the input power (I x V) and the output power (W / T) to thus determine the efficiency of the system. Our vehicle had the fastest time out of all the other vehicles in our course, taking only 2.82 seconds to travel 1 meter. My personal role in this project was building the vehicle, as well as modifying certain parts to maximize efficiency. For example, due to the size constraints, our original idea did not work as it would be too wide. As a result, I designed and built a solution of attaching standoffs to the top of the vehicle in order to mount the solar cells and the breadboard. Additionally, I worked on the actual frame of the vehicle as well. I personally built the chassis, as the motor mount, the sprocket system for driving the wheel, and modified other aspects of the vehicle to maximize efficiency. My partner and I explored and added bonus features to make the game more intriguing such as a life system, levels, timers, start/instructions screen, a cloud saving score, obstacles, and many more. In this project, I mainly did the programming as well as leave comments on my code which helped tremendously when troubleshooting problems such as overlapping moles/obstacles, screen errors, and lag. This helped me establish a great foundation of leaving comments, troubleshooting code, and patience (the longest bug to find was 2 days). Additionally, this experience taught me the importance of teamwork and communication as a team and that two brains are always better than one. Having more than one person look over code helps gives another perspective on to how to approach a problem. Overall, the project went pretty well and I am happy with how it turned out.
